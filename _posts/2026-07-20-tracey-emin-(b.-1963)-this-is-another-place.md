## The Work

"This Is Another Place" exemplifies Tracey Emin's enduring engagement with confessional textuality and emotional autobiography, likely executed in her characteristic appliqué or embroidered format on fabric or canvas. The title itself—declarative yet ambiguous—signals the introspective register that has defined her practice since the 1990s, where language functions as both subject and formal gesture. Works bearing such introspective titles typically emerge from periods of personal transition, suggesting this piece belongs to Emin's mature output where the rawness of her earlier work has crystallized into more formally refined but no less emotionally direct statements.

Within Emin's oeuvre, text-based works occupy a privileged position; they represent the artist's most direct translation of lived experience into visual form. "This Is Another Place" likely operates as a spatial or psychological meditation rather than a literal topographical reference, consistent with her exploration of displacement, memory, and emotional geography. The work would appeal to collectors seeking Emin pieces that balance conceptual rigor with visceral intimacy—a category increasingly recognized as foundational to late twentieth-century British art.

The extraordinary 620 percent premium above low estimate suggests the room identified a work of particular resonance or provenance strength. Such results typically reflect either impeccable institutional or notable private ownership, or a rare opportunity to acquire a thematically significant piece from a canonical period that seldom appears at auction.

## The Artist

Tracey Emin (b. 1963) is a British artist who emerged from the Young British Artists movement of the 1990s and has remained one of its most commercially durable figures. Born in Margate, Kent, she studied at the Royal College of Art in London during the mid-1980s, a period when conceptual practice was reasserting itself within British fine art education. Her formative years coincided with a broader cultural shift: the end of Thatcherism, the rise of Cool Britannia, and a generation of artists who treated confession, autobiography, and everyday materials as legitimate subjects for serious work.

Emin's emergence was contemporaneous with Damien Hirst, the Chapman brothers, and Sarah Lucas—artists who would collectively define the YBA movement through their embrace of provocation, accessibility, and market savvy. Where the 1980s had valued irony and appropriation, the 1990s YBAs offered rawness and vulnerability as radical propositions. Emin's breakthrough came through works that weaponized her own biography: "My Bed" (1998), the tent with embroidered names of her lovers ("Everyone I Have Ever Slept With 1963–1995"), and a body of neon text works that transformed intimate confessions into public spectacle. Critics initially dismissed her work as mere autobiography; she was defended by curators like Carl Freedman and Charles Saatchi, whose patronage proved decisive in legitimizing her practice within major institutions.

The market for YBAs peaked between 1995 and 2008, with Emin consistently outperforming her peers at auction. Her work maintained relatively stable valuations through the 2010s, though it never achieved the stratospheric prices commanded by Hirst's most iconic pieces. She benefited from institutional validation—major museum acquisitions, retrospectives, and her 2007 election as Royal Academician—which anchored her market position. The past decade has seen renewed interest in 1990s British art, as collectors reassessed the movement's cultural significance and as younger buyers discovered Emin's work outside the shadow of contemporary shock value.

This $288,036 result represents a significant spike but not unprecedented territory for her canvas works. Recent years have seen her achieve prices in the $200,000–$400,000 range at auction, particularly for works from the 1990s and early 2000s. This result confirms that demand for Emin remains robust and that her market has stabilized at a level substantially above where it stood in the 2010s. It signals collector appetite for YBA material at a moment when 1990s aesthetics hold renewed cultural currency—and when the biographical candor that once seemed transgressive now reads as foundational to contemporary practice.
