## The Result

Christie's specialists entered this sale with a $70,000–$100,000 estimate for Moore's "Seated Figure: Armless," a characteristically restrained valuation for a work by the twentieth century's most consistently collected sculptor. The hammer price of $114,300 represents a 63 percent premium over the low estimate, landing well above the high end and signaling genuine conviction in the room. This isn't marginal outperformance; it's the kind of spread that indicates multiple bidders were willing to move decisively past the house's conservative positioning.

The gap itself sits in familiar territory for Moore at auction. His work regularly exceeds modest estimates by 40–60 percent when the piece carries provenance weight and clean condition, which appears to have been the case here. What distinguishes this result from routine overperformance is the specific context: a day sale, not an evening session, where pricing typically reflects secondary-tier rather than flagship status. That dealers and collectors still pushed nearly 15 percent above the high estimate in this format suggests the work possessed either exceptional provenance or sufficient scarcity to justify premium positioning.

Moore's bronzes command this kind of attention because supply remains genuinely constrained. His foundry output ended decades ago, making each cast a finite asset. Collectors continue to view mid-scale bronzes like this one as accessible entry points to his oeuvre without sacrificing authenticity or market liquidity. The result indicates that patient bidders remain willing to pay for quality examples when they appear, even in secondary venues.

This performance confirms that Moore's market remains steady rather than speculative, rewarding selective examples without creating artificial momentum.

## The Work

"Seated Figure: Armless" exemplifies Moore's enduring preoccupation with the reclining and seated human form—a motif that animated his practice from the 1930s onward. This work, likely a bronze cast or maquette from the mid-twentieth century, distills the artist's signature vocabulary of organic abstraction into a single, compact statement. The absence of arms proves conceptually decisive: rather than a naturalistic omission, it forces the viewer's eye toward the torso's volumetric mass and the figure's psychological posture, anchoring Moore's investigation into how simplified forms can nonetheless convey human presence and vulnerability.

Within Moore's prolific output, seated variants occupy a particular register—less monumental than his celebrated reclining figures, yet more formally complex than preliminary sketches. This piece likely dates to a period when Moore was refining the relationship between void and solid, a concern that would intensify through the 1950s and 1960s. The armless constraint distinguishes it from his more elaborate seated bronzes, suggesting either an autonomous work of concentrated purpose or a stage in a larger series.

Collectors prize Moore works that demonstrate clear conceptual intent without sacrificing tactile presence. The hammer price—63 percent above the low estimate—reflects the room's recognition that this piece balances accessibility with the formal rigor that defines Moore's legacy. Its compact scale likely enhanced appeal for collectors with spatial constraints, while its thematic clarity positioned it as a confident entry point to the artist's oeuvre.

## The Artist

Henry Moore (1898–1986) was a British sculptor who became one of the twentieth century's most consequential figures in three-dimensional form. Born in Castleford, Yorkshire, Moore trained at Leeds School of Art and the Royal College of Art in London during the 1920s, where he encountered reproductions of African and pre-Columbian sculpture that would permanently reshape his aesthetic. He emerged as a leading voice in British modernism alongside Ben Nicholson and Barbara Hepworth, though Moore's work proved more commercially resilient and internationally legible than his contemporaries'.

Moore's career unfolded across distinct phases: the austere, abstracted reclining figures of the 1930s; the organic abstraction of the 1940s and 1950s, when he reached international prominence; and his increasingly monumental public commissions from the 1960s onward. He belonged to no single movement but synthesized Cubism, Surrealism, and a deeply personal vocabulary of voids and masses. The postwar years—particularly the 1950s through 1970s—marked his commercial and critical apotheosis, when major museums worldwide acquired his work and his bronze editions commanded premium prices.

Moore's auction market peaked in the late 1980s and early 1990s, shortly after his death, when speculative appetite for British modernism ran high. Prices have remained volatile since, with smaller bronzes and drawings experiencing periodic softness while major works and rare editions hold steady. Today he occupies a secure but not speculative tier: institutional collections treat him as essential, while collectors view him as reliable rather than undervalued.

This result—landing well above estimate for a mid-scale bronze—signals modest strength in the Moore market. It's neither a record nor a dramatic correction, but rather confirmation that disciplined works with clear provenance continue to find committed bidders at auction.
