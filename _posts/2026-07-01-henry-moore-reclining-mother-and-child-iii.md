## The Result

Christie's specialists entered this sale with a $70,000–$100,000 estimate on "Reclining Mother and Child III," positioning the work in the lower-middle range of Moore's cast bronze output. The hammer price of $170,100 represents a 143 percent jump from the low estimate and a 70 percent premium over the high end—a gap wide enough to signal genuine competition rather than a single determined buyer pushing past initial expectations. This is not a routine 10-to-15 percent overage; it's the kind of result that suggests the room saw something the pre-sale specialists either undervalued or underestimated in terms of current appetite.

For Moore's figurative bronzes, particularly the Reclining Mother and Child series, estimate inflation of this scale points to several converging pressures. The scarcity calculus matters—cast edition sizes in Moore's corpus vary, and collectors know which subjects cycle through auction infrequently. Institutional demand remains steady for Moore's postwar work, but private collectors have increasingly competed for strong examples as museum acquisition budgets tightened. The timing also tracks: collectors are rotating capital into mid-market modernist sculpture after years of chasing contemporary works, and Moore's foundational status in twentieth-century abstraction makes these pieces defensive holdings in uncertain conditions.

What this result actually signals is that Christie's estimate strategy—conservative enough to absorb upside—met a market more liquid and conviction-driven than the pre-sale view accounted for. Moore remains a reliable floor; the floor just moved up.

## The Work

"Reclining Mother and Child III" exemplifies Moore's most iconic sculptural subject rendered in bronze, likely dating from the 1960s or early 1970s—the artist's most prolific period for this theme. The work belongs to Moore's celebrated series exploring the relationship between maternal form and infant, a subject that occupied him from the 1930s onward but reached its fullest formal refinement during the postwar decades. The reclining posture, with its references to both Classical odalisques and modernist abstraction, allowed Moore to investigate volumetric mass, void, and the tension between figuration and pure form simultaneously.

What distinguishes this particular iteration—the third in a numbered sequence—is its refinement of a motif already proven successful. Moore's numbered variations typically represent deliberate explorations of compositional possibilities rather than simple repetition; each "III" suggests prior studies and considered evolution. The subject's domestic tenderness counterbalanced by monumental scale and abstracted anatomy made it deeply resonant with postwar audiences seeking humanistic values within modernism.

The substantial jump to $170,100—nearly 70 percent above the high estimate—reflects collector appetite for Moore's bronze work from this canonical period, particularly examples with clear provenance and established exhibition history. The maternal theme carries cultural weight beyond formal innovation, and bronze casts from this era remain finite, supporting sustained demand among both institutional buyers and seasoned private collectors navigating the secondary market.

## The Artist

Henry Moore (1898–1986) was a British sculptor and draughtsman whose international reputation rested on his invention of a modernist language for the human figure—particularly the reclining form. Born in Castleford, Yorkshire, Moore trained at Leeds School of Art and the Royal College of Art in London during the 1920s, where he absorbed both archaic sculpture and continental modernism. His early work showed the influence of pre-Columbian art and Brancusi's reductive vocabulary, but Moore synthesized these sources into something distinctly his own: biomorphic abstractions that retained a legible connection to the body, especially the female form at rest.

Moore's reclining figures, which emerged as his signature motif in the 1930s, positioned him within the broader avant-garde movement of European modernism, though his work maintained a humanistic core that distinguished it from pure abstraction. Contemporaries like Barbara Hepworth and Ben Nicholson occupied similar territory, but Moore achieved greater international visibility. By the 1950s and 1960s, he had become perhaps the most celebrated living sculptor in the West, with major public commissions and retrospectives cementing his canonical status.

The market for Moore's work has remained robust and relatively stable since his death, though prices peaked in the late 1980s and early 1990s during the contemporary art boom. Subsequent decades saw modest appreciation rather than dramatic growth; Moore occupies the upper tier of twentieth-century British sculpture but does not command the prices of his most celebrated bronzes at auction. This result—at 143 percent above low estimate—represents a significant outperformance for a work on paper, signaling renewed collector appetite for his graphic output, which has historically traded at a discount to his sculpture.
