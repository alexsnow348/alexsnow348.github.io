## The Work

"I promise to love you" exemplifies Tracey Emin's signature confessional mode, likely executed in neon—the medium that has become virtually synonymous with her practice since the 1990s. The work's declarative text, rendered in luminous tubing, positions intimacy and vulnerability as spectacle, a hallmark of Emin's investigation into the relationship between personal disclosure and public display. The piece belongs to her sustained engagement with written language as both formal gesture and emotional utterance, a body of work that emerged from her early video and installation practices and crystallized into her most commercially legible format.

Within Emin's oeuvre, neon works of romantic or emotional declaration occupy the center rather than the periphery of her project—these are not experiments but consolidations of her core concerns. A work addressing love and commitment carries particular weight given Emin's reputation as an artist willing to weaponize her own biography for aesthetic ends.

The hammer result, soaring 246 percent above the low estimate, suggests collectors recognized either institutional or provenance credentials not reflected in the presale estimate, or more likely, that this particular articulation of Emin's vocabulary—the marriage of formal elegance and emotional directness—resonated with sufficient demand to trigger competitive bidding. Neon works by Emin, especially those with thematic clarity and historical positioning within her practice, have proven to be among her most resilient market performers.

## The Artist

Tracey Emin (b. 1963) is a British artist whose career has been defined by radical confession and the weaponization of autobiography. Born in Margate, she trained at the Royal College of Art in the late 1980s—a period when London's art scene was fragmenting into competing camps of conceptualism, figuration, and identity politics. Emin's early work emerged from this ferment but refused to choose sides, instead collapsing the boundaries between personal trauma, feminist critique, and market spectacle. She remains active and prolific, with her work occupying an unusual position: simultaneously celebrated as a cornerstone of British contemporary art and dismissed by skeptics as mere provocation.

Emin belongs to the YBA (Young British Artists) generation that crystallized around Damien Hirst and Charles Saatchi's patronage in the early 1990s. But where Hirst dealt in deadpan shock and formalist rigor, Emin pioneered vulnerability as a medium. Her breakthrough came with works like "My Bed" (1998)—literally her unmade bed, festooned with detritus—which scandalized the Turner Prize shortlist and made her name synonymous with confessional art. Critics like Stuart Morgan recognized her work as extending the lineage of feminist performance art (Carolee Schneemann, Ana Mendieta) into the age of celebrity and spectacle. She was influenced equally by Pop Art's embrace of the personal and by the abject aesthetics emerging from 1980s feminist theory. Her embroidered text works, in particular, recall Jenny Holzer's deployment of language while remaining distinctly Emin—raw, misspelled, unguarded.

The auction market has tracked her reputation's undulations closely. The early 2000s saw rapid appreciation, with major museums acquiring her work and prices climbing steadily. By the mid-2000s, Emin had become a fixture at major auction houses, with contemporary sales regularly featuring her pieces. The 2008 financial crisis and subsequent market correction hit many YBA artists hard, but Emin's market proved more resilient than Hirst's. A secondary market correction in the early 2010s saw her prices plateau, though serious works continued to perform respectably. The last decade has brought something like critical rehabilitation—a recognition that her emotional directness and formal sophistication had been obscured by the shock-value narrative. Museum exhibitions (including a major retrospective at the Guggenheim in 2014) reinforced her canonical status.

This result—nearly 2.5 times the low estimate—signals that Emin's market has re-energized. "I promise to love you," a text-based work from 2000, sits squarely in her most iconic period. The hammer price represents a genuine high for this particular work and confirms that serious collectors now view her not as a 1990s provocation but as a foundational figure in contemporary art. The result suggests her market has stabilized at a new, elevated floor.
