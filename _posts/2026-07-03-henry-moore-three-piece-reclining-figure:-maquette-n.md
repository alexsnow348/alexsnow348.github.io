## The Work

# The Work

"Three Piece Reclining Figure: Maquette No. 1" represents Moore at the height of his formal investigations into the reclining figure—a motif that consumed him from the 1930s onward. As a maquette, this small-scale bronze study functioned as both autonomous artwork and conceptual blueprint for monumental public commissions, a distinction that elevates its status beyond preliminary sketch. The intimate scale paradoxically intensifies the work's sculptural language: the tripartite composition fragments the human form into abstracted volumes that suggest simultaneously a reclining body, landscape, and pure spatial relationships. This particular maquette likely dates to the postwar period, when Moore's practice of producing multiple bronze casts from plaster originals had become systematized, allowing the same work to exist in several numbered editions.

The three-piece configuration was not incidental to Moore's thinking but rather central to it—these fractured ensembles allowed him to explore how void and mass could dialogue across space, how negative space became as sculptural as positive form. For collectors, maquettes occupy a privileged position in Moore's oeuvre: they retain the immediacy of the artist's hand while commanding prices substantially below the monumental editions, yet they carry equivalent conceptual weight and market legitimacy. The hammer price's substantial leap above estimate signals collector appetite for works that demonstrate Moore's ideas in distilled, portable form—pieces suitable for serious collections but with the practical accessibility that monumental bronzes lack.

## The Artist

Henry Moore (1898–1986) stands as the twentieth century's most consequential British sculptor and arguably the defining figure in modernist abstraction applied to the human form. Born in Castleford, Yorkshire, to a mining family, Moore trained at the Leeds School of Art and the Royal College of Art in London during the 1920s, where he absorbed both the geometric vocabulary of Constructivism and the biomorphic language emerging from continental Europe. His career spanned seven decades of relentless formal innovation, making him perhaps the last of the true universal sculptors—equally commanding in monumental bronzes, intimate maquettes, drawings, and prints.

Moore's artistic genealogy runs through Henry Gaudier-Brzeska and directly to the Surrealist circle of the 1930s, though he resisted full membership in favor of a more independent path. His work synthesized Brancusi's reductive abstraction, primitive sculpture (particularly African and Pre-Columbian pieces he studied obsessively), and a distinctly British humanism that never fully abandoned the figure. Contemporaries like Barbara Hepworth pursued parallel investigations into abstraction and void, but Moore's work retained a tactile, almost archaeological quality—forms that seemed excavated rather than invented. By the 1940s, he had become the intellectual center of British modernism, celebrated by critics including Herbert Read, who positioned him as a counterweight to American Abstract Expressionism.

Moore's market trajectory has been remarkably stable, which is itself notable. Unlike many postwar artists whose prices have experienced boom-and-bust cycles, Moore benefited from sustained institutional support and consistent collector demand across both the British and American markets. His peak auction activity occurred in the 1980s and early 1990s, when major bronzes regularly exceeded $1 million. Prices recalibrated downward during the 2000s as supply increased and contemporary art commanded collector attention, but Moore never fell from favor—his work remains a cornerstone holding for serious sculptural collections. Today he occupies the upper tier of mid-twentieth-century sculpture, beneath only Rodin and Brancusi in historical weight, but with far superior auction liquidity than either.

This result represents a significant uptick for a maquette, even accounting for Moore's market status. The 278-percent jump over estimate signals strong bidding competition, likely driven by the work's documented provenance and the scarcity of early-period maquettes in excellent condition. Three Piece Reclining Figure series works, originating in 1961, represent Moore's most iconic motif—the recumbent form fragmented into abstract elements that somehow remain viscerally human. This particular maquette's price suggests renewed institutional and private appetite for Moore's foundational works, particularly pieces that demonstrate his formal vocabulary at its most resolved.
